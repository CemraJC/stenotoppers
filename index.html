About StenoToppers

A StenoTopper is a 3D printed keycap set that aims to optimize a mechanical keyboard for stenography - when used in conjunction with the Plover software. StenoToppers achieves this by modifying their height, offset and spacing to make stenotyping much more comfortable.

Aligns the rows

Less slant is more ergonomic

Reduces keycap tapering

Brings them closer, easier to chord

Raises the steno keys

No more painful spacebar dodging

Why you need StenoToppers

As it stands, QWERTY was designed to make it difficult to press multiple keys at once. Theoretically, this is good, but since the whole premise of the stenotype is to press multiple keys at once, we clearly have a problem. StenoToppers and other similar solutions (like the Laser-cut Keytoppers) are designed to counteract the problem with QWERTY's anti-chordal design.

Stenographers using Plover on a QWERTY keyboard tend to notice that the annoying and sometimes painful process of avoiding the spacebar, slanting your fingers (to line up with the keyboard's row staggering) and bending thumbs and pinkie fingers into unnatural shapes to overcome the substantial gap between keys. With StenoToppers, one can align the rows, raise them above the spacebar and move the keys closer in one fell swoop. StenoToppers help you reclaim your ergonomic advantage.

How to print some

Currently, StenoToppers are only available through 3D printing. If you already have access to a 3D printer, then you should be able to download the models from above and print them for negligible cost (about $3 worth of filament, duration of 12 hours).

If, like the rest of us, you don't have access to a 3D printer, you will need to utilize a 3D printing service like Shapeways (costs about $65, then shipping). When using Shapeways, you can upload and configure the models yourself or you can use the ones already uploadedfor free (well, there is the print cost).

Here is an overview of the steps to print some StenoToppers on your own printer:

  1. Download the models zip file above
  2. Locate the standard-keycap.stl file
  3. Load this file into your slicing software and do a draft-quality test print
  4. Confirm that the keycap fits properly. If not, make tweaks with a mesh editor (or calibrate your printer) and go back to step 3
  5. Print another draft and confirm that they align properly
  6. If all is well, locate the final-print.stl assembly and print it at your desired quality.
